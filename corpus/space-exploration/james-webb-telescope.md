# The James Webb Space Telescope: A New Window on the Universe

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is the most powerful space telescope ever built, designed to observe the universe primarily in infrared wavelengths. A joint project of NASA, the European Space Agency (ESA), and the Canadian Space Agency (CSA), JWST was launched on December 25, 2021, aboard an Ariane 5 rocket from Kourou, French Guiana. After a 30-day journey, it reached its operational orbit at the Sun-Earth Lagrange Point 2 (L2), approximately 1.5 million km from Earth, where it maintains a stable position relative to both the Sun and the Earth.

## Design and Instruments

JWST's primary mirror is 6.5 meters in diameter, composed of 18 hexagonal gold-coated beryllium segments. This gives it a collecting area approximately six times larger than the Hubble Space Telescope's 2.4-meter mirror. To observe faint infrared signals, the telescope must be kept extremely cold; a five-layer sunshield the size of a tennis court keeps the instruments at approximately minus 233 degrees Celsius (40 Kelvin), with the MIRI instrument cooled further to minus 266 degrees Celsius (7 Kelvin) by an active cryocooler.

JWST carries four primary science instruments:

**NIRCam (Near-Infrared Camera)** is the primary imaging instrument, covering wavelengths from 0.6 to 5 micrometers. Built by the University of Arizona, it also serves as the wavefront sensor for aligning the mirror segments.

**NIRSpec (Near-Infrared Spectrograph)** can observe up to 200 objects simultaneously using a micro-shutter array containing 250,000 tiny shutters. Built by ESA, it operates from 0.6 to 5.3 micrometers and is critical for studying the chemical composition of distant galaxies and exoplanet atmospheres.

**MIRI (Mid-Infrared Instrument)** covers wavelengths from 5 to 28 micrometers, providing both imaging and spectroscopy. It is uniquely capable of peering through dense dust clouds to observe star and planet formation in regions invisible to shorter-wavelength instruments.

**FGS/NIRISS (Fine Guidance Sensor / Near-Infrared Imager and Slitless Spectrograph)** provides precise pointing and also functions as a science instrument optimized for exoplanet transit spectroscopy and wide-field imaging.

## Key Discoveries

JWST's first full-color images were released on July 12, 2022, and the telescope has since transformed multiple fields of astronomy.

**Early Universe and Distant Galaxies:** JWST has detected galaxies existing within 300 million years of the Big Bang, far earlier than previous models predicted. Galaxies such as JADES-GS-z14-0, observed at a redshift of approximately 14, challenge existing theories of galaxy formation by appearing more massive and structured than expected at such early epochs.

**Exoplanet Atmospheres:** JWST has produced the first detailed atmospheric spectra of exoplanets, detecting water vapor, carbon dioxide, sulfur dioxide, and even silicate clouds in the atmospheres of gas giants and smaller worlds. Its observations of planets in the TRAPPIST-1 system, a key target in exoplanet science, are probing whether these rocky worlds retain atmospheres. Transit spectroscopy with NIRSpec and NIRISS is helping identify which exoplanets might have conditions suitable for life by analyzing atmospheric biosignatures such as ozone, methane, and water in combination.

**Star Formation:** JWST's infrared capabilities have revealed previously hidden details within stellar nurseries like the Carina Nebula and the Pillars of Creation. MIRI observations penetrate the dust to show protostars in their earliest stages, providing data on how stars and planetary systems form from collapsing molecular clouds.

**Solar System Science:** JWST has also observed objects within our own solar system, capturing detailed images of Jupiter's auroras, Saturn's rings, and Uranus's faint ring system. It has detected carbon dioxide frost and organic molecules on distant Kuiper Belt objects, contributing to our understanding of the solar system's outer reaches.

## Comparison to Hubble

While Hubble primarily observes in ultraviolet and visible light (0.1 to 0.8 micrometers), JWST operates in the infrared (0.6 to 28 micrometers). This makes JWST far superior for observing the most distant, redshifted objects, seeing through dust clouds, and characterizing exoplanet atmospheres. However, Hubble remains valuable for ultraviolet observations that JWST cannot perform. The two telescopes have conducted complementary observations, with Hubble identifying targets that JWST then examines in greater infrared detail.

## Mission Outlook

JWST was designed for a minimum five-year mission with a goal of ten years, and the precision of its launch trajectory conserved enough fuel for an estimated twenty-year operational lifetime. Its observations are accelerating discoveries in cosmology, galactic evolution, star formation, and planetary science, fundamentally reshaping our understanding of the universe from its earliest moments to the planetary systems forming today.
