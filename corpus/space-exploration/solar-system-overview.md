# The Solar System: An Overview

Our solar system formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust. It consists of eight planets, five recognized dwarf planets, hundreds of moons, and countless smaller bodies including asteroids and comets. The Sun, a G-type main-sequence star, contains 99.86% of the system's total mass and provides the gravitational anchor for all orbiting bodies.

## The Inner Planets

The four inner planets are rocky, terrestrial worlds with solid surfaces.

**Mercury** is the smallest planet with a diameter of 4,879 km, orbiting at an average distance of 57.9 million km from the Sun. Its orbital period is just 88 Earth days. Despite its proximity to the Sun, Mercury is not the hottest planet; it lacks a substantial atmosphere to trap heat, so temperatures swing from 430 degrees Celsius on the sunlit side to minus 180 degrees Celsius at night.

**Venus** has a diameter of 12,104 km and orbits at 108.2 million km from the Sun with a period of 225 Earth days. Its thick carbon dioxide atmosphere creates a runaway greenhouse effect, making it the hottest planet at 465 degrees Celsius. Venus rotates backwards compared to most planets, and its day is longer than its year.

**Earth** measures 12,742 km in diameter, orbits at 149.6 million km (1 AU), and completes one orbit in 365.25 days. It is the only known planet to support life, with liquid water covering 71% of its surface and a nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere.

**Mars**, the Red Planet, has a diameter of 6,779 km and orbits at 227.9 million km from the Sun with a period of 687 Earth days. Its thin carbon dioxide atmosphere and iron oxide surface give it a distinctive reddish appearance. Mars hosts the tallest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, at 21.9 km high. Numerous missions have explored Mars in search of water and signs of past life, as detailed in the Mars exploration record.

## The Outer Planets

The four outer planets are gas and ice giants with no solid surfaces.

**Jupiter** is the largest planet at 139,820 km in diameter, orbiting at 778.5 million km with a period of 11.86 Earth years. It has at least 95 known moons, including the four Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. Europa is considered one of the most promising places to search for extraterrestrial life due to its subsurface ocean.

**Saturn** measures 116,460 km in diameter and orbits at 1.43 billion km with a period of 29.46 years. It is famous for its extensive ring system, composed primarily of ice particles and rocky debris. Saturn's moon Titan has a thick nitrogen atmosphere and liquid methane lakes on its surface.

**Uranus** has a diameter of 50,724 km and orbits at 2.87 billion km with a period of 84 years. It rotates on its side with an axial tilt of 97.8 degrees, likely the result of a massive ancient collision. It was the first planet discovered with a telescope, by William Herschel in 1781.

**Neptune** is 49,244 km in diameter, orbiting at 4.50 billion km with a period of 164.8 years. It has the strongest winds in the solar system, reaching speeds of 2,100 km/h. Neptune's moon Triton orbits in a retrograde direction, suggesting it was captured from the Kuiper Belt.

## Dwarf Planets and Small Bodies

The International Astronomical Union recognizes five dwarf planets: Pluto, Eris, Haumea, Makemake, and Ceres. Pluto, reclassified in 2006, resides in the Kuiper Belt and was visited by the New Horizons spacecraft in 2015, revealing a geologically active world with nitrogen ice glaciers. Ceres is the largest object in the asteroid belt and was explored by the Dawn spacecraft.

The **asteroid belt** lies between Mars and Jupiter, spanning roughly 2.2 to 3.2 AU from the Sun. It contains millions of rocky bodies, though its total mass is less than 4% of the Moon's mass. Notable asteroids include Vesta and Pallas.

The **Kuiper Belt** extends from Neptune's orbit at about 30 AU to approximately 50 AU. It is home to thousands of icy bodies and is the source of many short-period comets. Beyond the Kuiper Belt lies the hypothetical Oort Cloud, a spherical shell of icy objects that may extend up to 100,000 AU from the Sun and is thought to be the source of long-period comets.

Understanding the architecture of our solar system also informs the search for planets around other stars, known as exoplanets, which modern telescopes like the James Webb Space Telescope are now characterizing in unprecedented detail.
