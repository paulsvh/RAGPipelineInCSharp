# Exoplanet Discoveries: Finding Worlds Beyond Our Solar System

An exoplanet is any planet that orbits a star other than our Sun. Once purely hypothetical, exoplanets are now known to be extraordinarily common. As of early 2025, over 5,700 exoplanets have been confirmed, with thousands more candidates awaiting verification. The study of exoplanets has become one of the most dynamic fields in astronomy, driven by increasingly powerful detection methods and observatories such as the James Webb Space Telescope, which can now analyze the atmospheres of these distant worlds.

## Detection Methods

Because exoplanets are vastly fainter than their host stars, direct observation is extremely challenging. Most discoveries rely on indirect techniques.

**Transit Method:** When a planet passes in front of its star as seen from Earth, it blocks a small fraction of the starlight, causing a periodic dip in brightness. The depth of the dip reveals the planet's size relative to the star, and the period between transits gives the orbital period. NASA's Kepler Space Telescope, launched in 2009, used this method to discover over 2,600 confirmed exoplanets by monitoring more than 150,000 stars simultaneously. Its successor, the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), launched in 2018, surveys nearly the entire sky and focuses on nearby bright stars. The transit method is most effective for planets with orbits aligned edge-on relative to Earth, which introduces a geometric selection bias.

**Radial Velocity (Doppler) Method:** A planet's gravitational pull causes its host star to wobble slightly, producing periodic shifts in the star's spectral lines. This method was used to confirm the first exoplanet orbiting a Sun-like star, 51 Pegasi b, by Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz in 1995, a discovery that earned them the 2019 Nobel Prize in Physics. Radial velocity measurements are most sensitive to massive planets on close-in orbits and can determine a planet's minimum mass.

**Direct Imaging:** By using coronagraphs to block the star's light, telescopes can sometimes photograph the planet itself. This method works best for large, young, hot planets on wide orbits. Notable directly imaged systems include HR 8799, where four giant planets were captured orbiting the star. Future missions such as NASA's Habitable Worlds Observatory aim to directly image Earth-like planets around nearby stars.

**Other Methods:** Gravitational microlensing detects planets when a foreground star and its planet bend the light of a background star. Astrometry measures the tiny positional shifts of a star caused by an orbiting planet. Transit timing variations reveal additional planets in a system by detecting changes in the timing of a known planet's transits.

## Notable Exoplanets

**Proxima Centauri b** orbits Proxima Centauri, the nearest star to the Sun at 4.24 light-years. Discovered in 2016 via radial velocity, it has a minimum mass of approximately 1.17 Earth masses and orbits within its star's habitable zone with a period of 11.2 days. However, Proxima Centauri is an active red dwarf that emits frequent powerful flares, raising questions about whether the planet could retain an atmosphere or support liquid water.

**The TRAPPIST-1 System** is a remarkable collection of seven roughly Earth-sized planets orbiting an ultracool red dwarf star 40 light-years away, discovered between 2016 and 2017. Three of these planets, TRAPPIST-1e, f, and g, lie within the habitable zone. The system is a prime target for atmospheric characterization by the James Webb Space Telescope, which has already begun transit spectroscopy observations to determine whether these worlds have atmospheres and what gases they contain.

**Kepler-442b** orbits a K-type star about 1,206 light-years away. With a radius 1.34 times Earth's and an orbital period of 112 days, it resides well within the habitable zone. It is one of the most promising candidates for a rocky planet with conditions potentially suitable for liquid water.

## The Habitable Zone

The habitable zone, sometimes called the Goldilocks zone, is the range of orbital distances from a star where conditions might allow liquid water to exist on a planet's surface. The boundaries depend on the star's luminosity and the planet's atmospheric composition. For our Sun, the habitable zone extends roughly from 0.95 to 1.67 AU. However, habitability depends on many additional factors including atmospheric pressure, magnetic field strength, tidal locking, and stellar activity.

## Atmospheric Biosignatures

Detecting signs of life on exoplanets requires analyzing their atmospheric composition through spectroscopy, particularly transit spectroscopy where starlight filters through a planet's atmosphere. Potential biosignatures include the simultaneous presence of oxygen and methane, which are chemically incompatible and would require a continuous biological source. Ozone, nitrous oxide, and dimethyl sulfide are also considered possible indicators of biological activity. The James Webb Space Telescope's NIRSpec and NIRISS instruments are currently conducting transit spectroscopy of promising targets, though detecting biosignatures on small rocky planets remains at the edge of JWST's capabilities. Future dedicated missions may be required to conclusively identify signs of life beyond Earth.
