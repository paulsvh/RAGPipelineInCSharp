# Mars Exploration: Missions, Discoveries, and the Path Forward

Mars has been a primary target of planetary exploration for over half a century. With a diameter of 6,779 km, a thin carbon dioxide atmosphere averaging about 6 millibars of surface pressure, average temperatures around minus 60 degrees Celsius, and a day length (sol) of 24 hours and 37 minutes, Mars is the most Earth-like planet in the solar system and a leading candidate for eventual human settlement.

## Early Missions: Viking Program

NASA's Viking program represented the first successful landing on Mars. Viking 1 touched down on July 20, 1976, in Chryse Planitia, followed by Viking 2 on September 3, 1976, in Utopia Planitia. Each mission consisted of an orbiter and a lander. The landers carried biology experiments designed to detect metabolic activity in Martian soil. The results were ambiguous: the Labeled Release experiment showed positive results, but the Gas Chromatograph Mass Spectrometer found no organic molecules, leading most scientists to conclude the results were caused by chemical rather than biological reactions. The orbiters mapped the surface extensively and captured images of Valles Marineris, a canyon system stretching over 4,000 km.

## Pathfinder and Sojourner

Mars Pathfinder landed on July 4, 1997, deploying Sojourner, the first rover to operate on another planet. Weighing just 10.6 kg, Sojourner analyzed rocks near the landing site in Ares Vallis using an Alpha Proton X-ray Spectrometer. The mission confirmed that the landing area had once experienced catastrophic flooding. Pathfinder demonstrated a low-cost approach to planetary exploration and pioneered the airbag landing system.

## Spirit and Opportunity

The Mars Exploration Rovers, Spirit and Opportunity, landed in January 2004. Spirit explored Gusev Crater, finding evidence of past hydrothermal activity and minerals formed in the presence of water. It operated until 2010 when it became stuck in soft soil. Opportunity landed in Meridiani Planum and discovered hematite spherules (nicknamed "blueberries"), cross-bedded sandstone, and mineral deposits confirming that liquid water once flowed across the surface. Opportunity far exceeded its 90-day planned mission, operating for over 14 years and covering 45.16 km before a planet-wide dust storm ended its mission in 2018.

## Curiosity Rover

NASA's Mars Science Laboratory mission delivered the Curiosity rover to Gale Crater on August 6, 2012, using the innovative sky crane landing system. Curiosity is a car-sized rover powered by a radioisotope thermoelectric generator rather than solar panels. Key discoveries include detection of organic molecules in mudstone rocks, seasonal variations in atmospheric methane, evidence that Gale Crater once held a lake system that could have supported microbial life, and radiation measurements critical for planning future human missions. Curiosity continues to climb Mount Sharp, analyzing increasingly ancient rock layers to understand how Mars transitioned from a warmer, wetter world to the cold desert it is today.

## Perseverance and Ingenuity

Perseverance landed in Jezero Crater on February 18, 2021, a site chosen because it contains an ancient river delta. The rover carries the SHERLOC and PIXL instruments for detecting organic compounds and analyzing elemental composition at fine scales. A key objective is collecting and caching rock samples for eventual return to Earth by a future Mars Sample Return mission. Perseverance also deployed the Ingenuity helicopter, which achieved the first powered, controlled flight on another planet on April 19, 2021. Originally planned for five flights, Ingenuity completed 72 flights over nearly three years, demonstrating that aerial exploration of Mars is viable.

## The Search for Water

Evidence for water on Mars has accumulated across multiple missions. Orbital observations have revealed recurring slope lineae, polar ice caps containing both water and carbon dioxide ice, and underground ice deposits detected by radar instruments. The Mars Express orbiter identified a possible subsurface lake beneath the south polar ice cap in 2018, though this interpretation remains debated. Subsurface water ice at accessible depths is considered essential for any future human presence on Mars.

## Future Human Missions

Multiple organizations are planning crewed missions to Mars. NASA's Artemis program aims to use lunar missions as a stepping stone for Mars exploration. SpaceX is developing the Starship vehicle, a fully reusable launch system using Raptor engines burning liquid methane and liquid oxygen, chosen partly because methane can be synthesized on Mars from atmospheric carbon dioxide and subsurface water via the Sabatier reaction. Estimated transit times using chemical propulsion are six to nine months; nuclear thermal propulsion concepts could reduce this to roughly four months. Challenges include radiation exposure during transit, the long communication delay of 4 to 24 minutes one-way, and the need for in-situ resource utilization to produce fuel, water, and breathable oxygen on the Martian surface.
