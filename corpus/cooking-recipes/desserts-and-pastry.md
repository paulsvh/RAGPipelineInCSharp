# Desserts and Pastry

Pastry and dessert work demands precision. Unlike savory cooking, where you can adjust seasonings on the fly, baking and confection rely on exact ratios, controlled temperatures, and careful timing. The following recipes and techniques cover foundational skills that apply across the entire dessert repertoire.

## Chocolate Mousse

**Servings:** 6 | **Prep time:** 30 minutes plus 4 hours chilling | **Difficulty:** Moderate

### Ingredients
- 200g dark chocolate (70% cocoa), finely chopped
- 4 large eggs, separated
- 30g granulated sugar
- 240ml heavy cream, cold
- Pinch of salt
- 1 teaspoon vanilla extract

### Instructions
1. Melt the chocolate gently using a double boiler (a heatproof bowl set over a pot of barely simmering water — the bowl should not touch the water). Stir until smooth. Remove from heat and let cool for 5 minutes.
2. Whisk the egg yolks into the melted chocolate one at a time until fully incorporated. Add vanilla extract.
3. In a clean, dry bowl, whip the egg whites with a pinch of salt until soft peaks form. Gradually add the sugar and continue whipping to stiff, glossy peaks.
4. In a separate bowl, whip the heavy cream to soft peaks.
5. Fold one-third of the egg whites into the chocolate mixture to lighten it. Then gently fold in the remaining whites in two additions, using a large spatula and turning the bowl as you fold. Finally, fold in the whipped cream.
6. Divide among serving glasses or ramekins. Refrigerate for at least 4 hours, or overnight.

**Key technique — Folding:** The goal is to incorporate air-rich mixtures without deflating them. Cut the spatula down through the center of the mixture, sweep along the bottom, and bring it up and over. Rotate the bowl a quarter turn and repeat. Work quickly but gently. Over-mixing produces a dense mousse instead of a light, airy one.

## Creme Brulee

**Servings:** 6 | **Prep time:** 20 minutes | **Cook time:** 45-50 minutes plus 4 hours chilling | **Difficulty:** Moderate

### Ingredients
- 600ml heavy cream
- 1 vanilla bean (or 2 teaspoons vanilla extract)
- 6 large egg yolks
- 100g granulated sugar, plus extra for the brulee topping

### The Custard Ratio
A classic creme brulee follows a ratio of roughly 1 cup (240ml) of cream to 3 egg yolks and 2-3 tablespoons of sugar. This ratio produces a custard that is rich, silky, and just firm enough to hold its shape when unmolded but trembles when nudged. Understanding this ratio lets you scale the recipe freely.

### Instructions
1. Preheat oven to 150C/300F. Place six ramekins in a deep baking dish.
2. Split the vanilla bean lengthwise and scrape out the seeds. Combine cream, vanilla seeds, and the scraped pod in a saucepan. Heat over medium until the cream just begins to steam and tiny bubbles appear at the edges — do not boil. Remove from heat and steep for 10 minutes.
3. Whisk the egg yolks and sugar together until pale and smooth. Slowly pour the warm cream into the yolk mixture, whisking constantly. This is called tempering — it gradually raises the temperature of the yolks so they do not curdle from thermal shock (the same principle applies when making carbonara sauce, as noted in the Italian pasta dishes guide).
4. Strain the custard through a fine-mesh sieve to remove the vanilla pod and any bits of cooked egg. Divide among the ramekins.
5. Pour hot water into the baking dish until it reaches halfway up the sides of the ramekins (a water bath, or bain-marie). This insulates the custards from direct oven heat and ensures gentle, even cooking.
6. Bake for 45-50 minutes. The custards are done when they are set around the edges but still jiggle slightly in the center, like gelatin.
7. Remove from the water bath and cool to room temperature, then refrigerate for at least 4 hours.
8. To serve, sprinkle a thin, even layer of sugar over each custard. Caramelize with a kitchen torch, holding the flame 5-8cm from the surface and moving in circles until the sugar melts, bubbles, and turns amber. Let the sugar harden for 1 minute before serving.

## Pie Crust Techniques

A flaky, tender pie crust depends on keeping the fat cold and handling the dough as little as possible.

### Basic Pie Dough
- 300g all-purpose flour
- 1 teaspoon salt
- 1 tablespoon sugar
- 225g unsalted butter, cold, cut into 1cm cubes
- 80-100ml ice water

Cut the butter into the flour using a pastry blender or your fingertips until the mixture resembles coarse meal with some pea-sized butter pieces remaining. These irregular butter pieces are critical — they melt in the oven and create steam, which puffs the dough into flaky layers. Add ice water a tablespoon at a time, mixing with a fork, just until the dough holds together when squeezed. It should look shaggy, not smooth. Wrap in plastic and refrigerate for at least 1 hour.

### Blind Baking
Blind baking means pre-baking a pie crust before adding a wet filling (such as custard or cream pies). Without this step, the bottom crust stays soggy and raw.

1. Roll out the chilled dough and fit it into the pie dish. Trim and crimp the edges.
2. Line the dough with parchment paper and fill with pie weights, dried beans, or uncooked rice.
3. Bake at 190C/375F for 15-20 minutes until the edges are set and lightly golden.
4. Remove the weights and parchment. Prick the bottom with a fork to prevent bubbling.
5. Return to the oven for 8-12 minutes until the bottom is dry and golden.

### Tempering Chocolate
Tempering is the process of heating and cooling chocolate to stabilize its cocoa butter crystals. Properly tempered chocolate has a glossy sheen, a clean snap when broken, and a smooth mouthfeel. It is essential for chocolate decorations, truffles, and dipped confections.

1. Chop 300g chocolate finely — uniform pieces melt evenly.
2. Melt two-thirds (200g) in a double boiler to 50-55C/122-131F for dark chocolate (45-50C/113-122F for milk chocolate).
3. Remove from heat. Add the remaining one-third of chopped chocolate (the "seed") and stir continuously. The seed chocolate introduces stable cocoa butter crystals.
4. Stir until the temperature drops to 27-28C/80-82F for dark chocolate (26-27C/78-80F for milk).
5. Briefly reheat to the working temperature: 31-32C/88-90F for dark, 29-30C/84-86F for milk.
6. Test by spreading a thin layer on parchment. It should set within 5 minutes with a glossy finish and no streaks.

## Common Failures and Fixes

**Grainy custard:** The eggs were cooked too fast. Always temper yolks by adding hot liquid gradually. Bake custards in a water bath at low temperature.

**Tough pie crust:** Over-worked dough develops too much gluten. Mix until the dough just barely holds together. Use cold butter and cold water, and chill the dough before rolling.

**Chocolate seizes (turns grainy and stiff):** A small amount of moisture entered the melted chocolate. Chocolate and water are enemies in small quantities. If it seizes, stir in warm cream a tablespoon at a time to form a ganache — it cannot be returned to its original state, but the ganache is still useful.

**Mousse is too dense:** The base chocolate was too warm when the egg whites were folded in, causing them to deflate. Let melted chocolate cool to around 35-40C/95-104F before folding in aerated mixtures.
