# Spice and Herb Reference

Understanding spices and herbs transforms competent cooking into inspired cooking. This guide covers the most essential seasonings, their flavor profiles, best pairings, and storage advice.

## Spices

### Cumin
**Flavor profile:** Warm, earthy, slightly nutty with a faint citrus undertone. Cumin is a backbone spice in Mexican, Indian, Middle Eastern, and North African cuisines. It pairs naturally with coriander, chili powder, and turmeric. Toast whole cumin seeds in a dry skillet until fragrant before grinding for the deepest flavor. Ground cumin works well in dry rubs for grilled meats (see the grilling and smoking guide for rub techniques). Use it in chili, taco seasoning, hummus, and lentil soups.

### Coriander
**Flavor profile:** Citrusy, floral, mildly sweet. Coriander seed tastes completely different from fresh cilantro leaf, even though they come from the same plant. Ground coriander is a key component in curry powder blends and pairs beautifully with cumin, cardamom, and cinnamon. It enhances sausages, pickles, and roasted root vegetables.

### Paprika
**Flavor profile:** Ranges from sweet and mild (Hungarian sweet paprika) to smoky and intense (Spanish pimenton) to fiery hot. Sweet paprika adds color and subtle sweetness to stews, deviled eggs, and roasted potatoes. Smoked paprika is essential in barbecue rubs and pairs well with grilled chicken, pork, and vegetables. It is a natural companion to grilling — see the grilling and smoking guide for its role in dry rubs.

### Turmeric
**Flavor profile:** Earthy, slightly bitter, peppery, with a warm mustard-like quality. Turmeric is prized for its vibrant golden color as much as its flavor. It is foundational in curry powders, golden milk, and rice pilafs. Use it sparingly — too much turmeric turns dishes bitter and chalky. A quarter teaspoon is often sufficient. Pairs well with black pepper (which enhances absorption of curcumin, its active compound), ginger, coriander, and cumin.

### Cardamom
**Flavor profile:** Intensely aromatic, with notes of eucalyptus, mint, and lemon. Green cardamom is used in both sweet and savory applications — Indian chai, Scandinavian pastries, rice dishes, and curries. Black cardamom is smokier and more robust, suited to slow-cooked stews and braised meats. Crush whole pods lightly before adding to liquids. For baking, extract the seeds and grind them fresh. Pre-ground cardamom loses its potency quickly.

### Cinnamon
**Flavor profile:** Warm, sweet, woody. Ceylon cinnamon (true cinnamon) is delicate and complex. Cassia cinnamon (the more common variety) is bolder and more pungent. Cinnamon is versatile across sweet and savory dishes — it appears in desserts like chocolate mousse and creme brulee (see the desserts and pastry guide), as well as Moroccan tagines, Mexican mole, and spiced rice dishes.

### Red Pepper Flakes (Crushed Red Pepper)
**Flavor profile:** Sharp, direct heat with a faintly fruity quality. A staple in Italian-American cooking, red pepper flakes are the heat source in aglio e olio pasta (see the Italian pasta dishes guide). Add them early in cooking to bloom in oil, which distributes heat evenly. Add them late for concentrated bursts of spice.

## Herbs

### Basil
**Flavor profile:** Sweet, peppery, with hints of anise and clove. Fresh basil is the soul of Italian cooking — central to pesto, caprese salad, and Margherita pizza. It bruises easily, so tear leaves by hand rather than chopping, or use the chiffonade technique described in the knife skills guide. Add fresh basil at the very end of cooking to preserve its volatile oils. Dried basil is acceptable in long-cooked sauces but is a poor substitute for fresh in uncooked applications.

### Oregano
**Flavor profile:** Pungent, slightly bitter, earthy, with a peppery bite. Unlike basil, oregano actually improves with drying — dried oregano is more concentrated and is preferred in many cooked dishes. It is essential in Italian tomato sauces, Greek salads, and Mexican salsas. Pairs well with garlic, basil, thyme, and olive oil. Rub dried oregano between your palms before adding it to release its essential oils.

### Thyme
**Flavor profile:** Subtle, earthy, slightly floral, with a hint of mint. Thyme is one of the most versatile herbs, at home in French, Mediterranean, Caribbean, and Middle Eastern kitchens. It withstands long cooking times, making it ideal for stocks, braises, and roasts. Strip the tiny leaves from the woody stems before using — hold the top of the sprig and run your fingers downward against the grain. Fresh thyme branches are excellent in marinades for grilled meats.

### Rosemary
**Flavor profile:** Bold, piney, resinous, slightly camphor-like. Rosemary's assertive flavor can overpower delicate dishes, so use it judiciously. It pairs well with lamb, roasted potatoes, focaccia bread (see the bread baking basics guide), and roasted chicken. Mince it finely — the needle-like leaves can be unpleasant in large pieces. Rosemary-infused olive oil is a simple way to incorporate its flavor into dressings and dips.

### Parsley
**Flavor profile:** Clean, bright, slightly peppery. Flat-leaf (Italian) parsley has more flavor than curly parsley, which is primarily decorative. Parsley adds freshness as a finishing herb — stir it into pasta dishes like aglio e olio at the last moment, scatter it over grilled meats, or fold it into herb butter.

## Storage Tips

**Whole spices** retain potency for 2-3 years when stored in airtight containers away from heat, light, and moisture. **Ground spices** lose flavor more quickly — replace them every 6-12 months. To test freshness, rub a pinch between your fingers and smell it. If the aroma is faint, the spice is past its prime.

**Fresh herbs** should be stored upright in a glass of water in the refrigerator, loosely covered with a plastic bag. Basil is an exception — it turns black in the cold. Store basil at room temperature in water, like a bouquet of flowers.

**Whole vs. ground:** Whenever possible, buy whole spices and grind them as needed using a mortar and pestle or a dedicated spice grinder. Freshly ground cumin, coriander, and peppercorns have dramatically more flavor than their pre-ground counterparts.
