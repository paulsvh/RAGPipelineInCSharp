# Bread Baking Basics

Bread baking is one of the most rewarding skills a home cook can develop. Understanding hydration, fermentation, and oven behavior transforms flour and water into something remarkable.

## Sourdough Starter

A sourdough starter is a living culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria. It replaces commercial yeast and gives bread a complex, tangy flavor.

### Creating a Starter from Scratch
1. **Day 1:** Mix 50g whole wheat flour with 50g lukewarm water (about 27C/80F) in a clean jar. Stir well, cover loosely, and leave at room temperature.
2. **Day 2-5:** Every 24 hours, discard all but 50g of the mixture. Add 50g all-purpose flour and 50g water. Stir and cover.
3. **Day 6-10:** Switch to twice-daily feedings. Discard down to 25g, then add 50g flour and 50g water each time.
4. **Ready indicator:** The starter should double in volume within 4-6 hours of feeding, have a pleasant sour aroma, and show an airy, bubbly structure throughout.

### Maintaining Your Starter
For daily baking, keep it at room temperature and feed once or twice daily. For less frequent use, store it in the refrigerator and feed it once a week. Pull it out 2-3 days before you plan to bake and resume room-temperature feedings to reactivate it.

## Basic White Bread

**Yield:** 1 loaf | **Total time:** About 4 hours (including rising)

### Ingredients
- 500g bread flour (protein content 12-13%)
- 325g water (65% hydration)
- 10g salt
- 7g instant yeast (or 1 packet)

### Instructions
1. Combine flour and water in a large bowl. Mix until no dry flour remains. Let rest for 20 minutes (autolyse).
2. Add salt and yeast. Knead for 10-12 minutes by hand or 7 minutes in a stand mixer on medium speed until the dough passes the windowpane test — you should be able to stretch a small piece thin enough to see light through it without it tearing.
3. Shape into a ball, place in a lightly oiled bowl, and cover. Let rise at room temperature (22-24C/72-75F) until doubled, about 1-1.5 hours.
4. Punch down gently, shape into a loaf, and place in a greased 9x5 inch loaf pan. Cover and let rise again until the dough crowns about 2cm above the rim of the pan, about 45-60 minutes.
5. Preheat oven to 220C/425F. Slash the top of the loaf with a sharp blade or lame.
6. Bake for 25-30 minutes until the crust is deep golden brown and the internal temperature reads 93-96C/200-205F. Remove from pan immediately and cool on a wire rack for at least 30 minutes before slicing.

### Understanding Hydration
Hydration is the ratio of water to flour by weight. The basic white bread above is 65% hydration (325g water / 500g flour). Lower hydration (60-65%) produces a tighter, more manageable dough suited to sandwich loaves. Higher hydration (70-80%) creates an open, airy crumb with large irregular holes — typical of ciabatta and artisan loaves — but is more challenging to handle.

## Focaccia

**Yield:** 1 large sheet pan | **Total time:** 3 hours (including rising)

### Ingredients
- 500g bread flour
- 375g water (75% hydration)
- 10g salt
- 7g instant yeast
- 60ml extra-virgin olive oil, plus more for drizzling
- Flaky sea salt and fresh rosemary for topping

### Instructions
1. Mix flour, water, salt, and yeast into a shaggy dough. Add 30ml olive oil and incorporate. The dough will be wet and sticky — this is correct.
2. Instead of traditional kneading, perform a series of stretch-and-folds: every 30 minutes for 2 hours, pull one side of the dough up and fold it over itself. Rotate the bowl 90 degrees and repeat. Four folds per session, four sessions total.
3. Pour remaining olive oil onto a rimmed sheet pan and spread it around. Transfer the dough onto the pan and gently press it toward the edges. If it springs back, let it rest 10 minutes and try again.
4. Dimple the surface deeply with oiled fingertips. Drizzle generously with olive oil, scatter rosemary and flaky salt over the top.
5. Let rest for 30 minutes while you preheat the oven to 220C/425F.
6. Bake for 20-25 minutes until golden brown and crisp on the bottom.

## Troubleshooting Common Problems

**Dense, heavy crumb:** Usually caused by under-kneading (insufficient gluten development) or using flour with too little protein. Bread flour with 12-13% protein is ideal. Whole wheat flour absorbs more water, so increase hydration by 5-10% when substituting.

**Over-proofing:** If the dough rises too long, the gluten structure weakens and collapses. The loaf will be flat with a coarse, uneven texture. Poke the dough with a floured finger — if the indent springs back slowly but not completely, it is ready. If the indent stays and the dough feels slack, it is over-proofed. You can try to reshape it and let it proof again for a shorter time, but the final bread may be slightly inferior.

**Thick, pale crust:** Your oven temperature may be too low, or you may be baking in a dry environment. Steam during the first 10 minutes of baking helps the crust develop color and crispness. Place a pan of boiling water on the lower rack, or spritz the oven walls with a spray bottle.

**Gummy interior:** The bread was sliced too soon. Even after removing from the oven, starches continue to set as the bread cools. Wait at least 30 minutes — ideally an hour — before cutting into a fresh loaf.
