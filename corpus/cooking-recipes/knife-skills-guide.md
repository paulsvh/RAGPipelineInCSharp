# Knife Skills Guide

Good knife skills are the foundation of efficient cooking. Consistent cuts ensure even cooking times, improve presentation, and make prep work faster and safer.

## Essential Knife Types

**Chef's Knife (20-25cm / 8-10 inches):** The workhorse of the kitchen. Its curved blade allows a rocking motion ideal for mincing herbs, chopping vegetables, and slicing meat. If you own only one knife, make it a chef's knife.

**Paring Knife (8-10cm / 3-4 inches):** A small, precise blade for detailed work — peeling fruit, deveining shrimp, removing seeds, and trimming small vegetables.

**Serrated Bread Knife (25-30cm / 10-12 inches):** The saw-like teeth grip crusty surfaces without crushing the soft interior. Essential for slicing sourdough, focaccia, and other artisan breads (see bread baking basics). Also useful for slicing tomatoes.

**Nakiri (Japanese Vegetable Knife):** A flat, rectangular blade designed for precise vegetable work. Its straight edge makes full contact with the cutting board in a single downward motion, perfect for clean julienne and brunoise cuts.

**Boning Knife:** A thin, flexible blade that follows the contours of bone, used to break down whole chickens, debone cuts of meat, and fillet fish.

## Types of Cuts

### Julienne
Thin, matchstick-sized strips approximately 3mm x 3mm x 5-6cm (1/8 x 1/8 x 2-3 inches). First, trim the vegetable into a rectangular block. Slice lengthwise into 3mm planks, then stack the planks and cut them into 3mm strips. Julienne carrots and zucchini make excellent garnishes, stir-fry components, and salad toppings.

### Brunoise
A fine dice of approximately 3mm x 3mm x 3mm (1/8 inch cubes). Start with a julienne cut, then turn the strips 90 degrees and dice across them at 3mm intervals. Brunoise is used for aromatic bases, salsas, and garnishes. In the Bolognese recipe from the Italian pasta dishes collection, pancetta cut into a brunoise renders evenly and melts into the sauce.

### Chiffonade
A technique for cutting leafy herbs and greens into thin ribbons. Stack the leaves, roll them tightly into a cylinder, and slice across the roll with a sharp knife. This produces delicate ribbons of basil, mint, sage, or spinach. A chiffonade of fresh basil is a classic finishing touch for pasta and pizza.

### Mirepoix
Not a cut per se, but a foundational preparation: onion, carrot, and celery diced to a uniform size, typically 1cm (3/8 inch) cubes. The standard ratio is 2 parts onion to 1 part carrot to 1 part celery. Mirepoix forms the aromatic base of stocks, stews, braises, and sauces — including the Bolognese ragu described in the Italian pasta dishes guide. Uniform size matters because it ensures the vegetables soften at the same rate.

### Chop (Rough and Fine)
A rough chop produces irregular pieces of approximately 2cm (3/4 inch). A fine chop aims for pieces around 5mm (1/4 inch). Unlike a brunoise, precision is not critical — speed is the priority. Used for ingredients that will be pureed, stewed, or strained out.

### Mince
The finest non-mechanical cut. Garlic, shallots, and fresh herbs are most commonly minced. For garlic, crush the clove under the flat of your chef's knife to loosen the skin, then rock the blade back and forth over the clove, periodically gathering the pieces. For the aglio e olio pasta recipe, garlic is thinly sliced rather than minced to give it a milder, sweeter flavor.

## Sharpening Techniques

**Honing Rod (Steel):** A honing rod does not remove metal — it realigns the microscopic edge of the blade that bends during normal use. Hold the rod vertically with the tip resting on a towel. Draw the knife downward at a 15-20 degree angle, alternating sides. Do this before every cooking session.

**Whetstone:** A whetstone actually sharpens the blade by grinding away metal to create a new edge. Soak the stone in water for 10-15 minutes. Place it on a damp towel to prevent slipping. Hold the knife at a consistent 15 degree angle (for Japanese knives) or 20 degrees (for Western knives). Push the blade away from you across the stone in a sweeping motion, maintaining consistent pressure. Work through progressively finer grits — start at 1000 grit, finish at 3000-6000 grit.

**Professional Sharpening:** If you are uncomfortable with a whetstone, have your knives professionally sharpened once or twice a year and use a honing rod between sharpenings.

## Safety Tips

- **Always cut on a stable surface.** Place a damp towel under your cutting board to prevent it from sliding.
- **Curl your fingers.** Use the "claw grip" — tuck your fingertips under and let your knuckles guide the blade. The flat of the knife should rest against your knuckles, never your fingertips.
- **A sharp knife is a safe knife.** A dull blade requires more force, increasing the chance it will slip off the food and into your hand.
- **Never catch a falling knife.** Step back and let it drop. Pick it up after it lands.
- **Cut away from your body.** When using a paring knife for detail work, draw the blade away from yourself.
- **Keep your workspace clean.** Clear scraps, wipe up spills, and never leave a knife hidden under a towel or submerged in soapy water where someone might reach in blindly.
